# Public Entry Intake Pack Example

This is a fictional example. It does not claim real market proof and does not recommend building a product.

Pain ID: EX-PACK-001

Pain Name: Repeated Team Note Drift

Raw Pain:

A small team keeps losing the reason behind weekly decisions because notes are scattered across meeting documents, chat threads, and task comments.

Evidence:

- Fictional user interview note: "We know what we decided, but not why."
- Fictional support pattern: repeated requests for decision summaries before project reviews.
- Fictional workflow observation: team members copy old notes into new documents without checking whether they are still current.

Source Type:

Fictional composite example.

Observed Audience:

Small project teams using manual notes and chat-based coordination.

Target Audience:

Team leads, operators, and project owners who need decision continuity without adding heavy process.

Timing Curve:

- Now: Annoying but often handled manually.
- 1M: Pain increases when multiple decisions overlap.
- 3M: Repeated re-explanation becomes visible as review overhead.
- 6M: Teams may look for lightweight decision-memory workflows.
- 12M: Stronger need if cross-tool memory and review workflows become common.

Pain Market Signal:

Small repeated coordination pain around why a decision was made, not only what the decision was.

Pain Intensity:

Medium.

Visible Population:

Small teams with recurring projects and many lightweight decisions.

Market Size:

Unknown.

Willingness to Pay:

Unproven.

Unsolvedness:

Partially solved by docs, task tools, and meeting summaries, but decision reason continuity is still easy to lose.

Growth Potential:

Possible if teams rely more on AI-generated summaries and need stronger source/time anchors.

LLM Absorption Risk:

Medium. Generic AI note summaries may absorb part of the pain, but may not preserve decision responsibility or timing context.

Existing Alternatives:

Meeting notes, task comments, wikis, decision logs, and AI summarizers.

Differentiation Need:

The package would need clear evidence that users need decision-reason continuity, not just better summaries.

User Fit:

Potential fit for operators who already maintain decision logs or project retrospectives.

Avoidance Conflict:

Users may avoid adding another documentation step.

Cost / Expense Burden:

Low to medium. Mostly time and process burden.

Build Burden:

Unknown. Pain Timing Map does not decide whether to build.

Sales Friction:

Likely high unless the pain is tied to concrete review costs or repeated restart burden.

Time-to-Cash:

Unknown.

Commercial Recovery:

- Repeat Commitment Threshold: A team repeatedly asks for "why did we decide this?" across several reviews.
- Main Drop-off Point: Users stop filling structured decision notes.
- Leverage Contact: Project owner or team lead.
- Retention / Payment Signal: Repeated use before weekly or monthly reviews.
- Recovery Risk: Existing tools may be enough if the pain is mild.

Entry-Relevant Summary:

This fictional signal suggests a possible pain around decision-reason continuity. It is not enough to justify entry by itself.

What Entry Should Evaluate Next:

- Whether the pain repeats across teams.
- Whether users already pay in time, process, or tools to solve it.
- Whether the need survives generic AI summaries.
- Whether the user wants a lightweight review workflow or just better notes.

What Pain Does Not Decide:

Pain does not decide whether to enter, build, price, sell, or automate this workflow.

Confidence:

Low. Fictional example only.

Remaining Unknowns:

- Real audience size.
- Real willingness to pay.
- Existing tool satisfaction.
- Frequency of repeated decision-reason loss.

Responsibility Boundary:

This package prepares evidence-backed material for later Entry evaluation. It is not an Entry Decision or Build Command.
